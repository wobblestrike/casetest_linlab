Detection of Biomolecules through Surface-Enhanced Raman Spectroscopy

Surface-enhanced Raman spectroscopy (SERS) is a powerful analytical tool and is used when molecules are adsorbed on rough metal surfaces or on aggregated metal nanoparticles. Enhancement in SERS of up to single-molecule detection is theoretically possible and has been demonstrated in some cases by applying sophisticated techniques. Not surprisingly, attention has turned toward the development of SERS for use as an ultrasensitive platform for the detection of biomolecules or metal ions in particular. Among the various SERS-active metal substrates, Au and Ag are used most widely. Relative to Ag nanoparticles, Au nanoparticles have the beneficial properties of long-term stability and a narrow size distribution. Nevertheless, the enhancement of molecules adsorbed onto Au nanoparticles are usually 100−1000 times lower than those adsorbed onto Ag nanoparticles. To take advantage of Au and Ag nanomaterials for SERS applications, Ag/Au nanoparticles and nanowires have been previously reported. However, the reproducibility and enhancement factors of SERS signals using such nanostructures are challenged. Our aim in this research is to develop new SERS substrates with strong and reproducible SERS signals, which can be useful for the detection of biomolecules or metal ions in real samples. For example, We have developed a simple and sensitive assay—using Au ‘‘pearl necklace’’ nanomaterials as the SERS substrate and albumin blue 580 as the reporter—for the determination of human serum albumin (HSA) in urine. This new SERS approach is sensitive, providing an LOD for HSA detection of 70 pM. Relative to the use of spherical Au nanoparticles as the substrate, Au ‘‘pearl necklace’’ nanomaterials provide superior reproducibility and greater SERS signal enhancements. With its high sensitivity and reproducibility, our newly developed SERS approach holds great potential for practical application in the analysis of biomolecules or metal ions in real samples.
