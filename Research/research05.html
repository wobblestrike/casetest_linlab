Preparation of Functional Nanomaterials with Strong Antibacterial Activities

Pathogenic bacteria that cause a large number of diseases and high death rate each year remain a major health concern. Although numerous antibacterial agents have been synthesized and even commercialized, they tend to be expensive, unstable, too specific, and/or toxic. Thus, cheap and environmentally friendly agents that allow effective killing of pathogenic bacteria are still urgently required. Antibacterial agents are either organic or inorganic compounds. Compared to organic antibacterial reagents, inorganic reagents such as metals and metal oxides are robust and durable, and have become more popular in recent years. Metal oxide nanoparticles such as titanium dioxide (TiO2), silicon dioxide (SiO2), and zinc oxide (ZnO) display strong biocidal activity against both Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria. These nanoparticles have a relatively large surface area and can generate reactive oxygen species (ROS) under high-intensity light at a specific wavelength. Among them, only TiO2 nanoparticles exhibit antibacterial activity under sunlight illumination. Unfortunately, the antibacterial activity of pure TiO2 nanoparticles is marginal. In addition, the photocatalytic sterilization of TiO2 nanoparticles is highly restricted, mainly because of a low electron transfer rate to oxygen adsorbed on their surfaces and a high electron–hole recombination rate. Alternatively, various Ag nanomaterials have been demonstrated to have antibacterial properties with the major advantage of outstanding broad-spectrum antimicrobial activity. Although Ag nanomaterials are effective, they are reportedly toxic to human cells. In this research, we have developed Te, Ag2Te, Au–Ag2Te, and Se/Te-TiO2 nanomaterials as new antibacterial agents. The antibacterial activities of those nanomaterials against E. coli are high (live/dead ratio < 0.1). The most exciting result is that the toxicity of those nanomaterials toward mammalian cells (LLC-PK1) is lower than that of synthesized Ag nanoparticles. With high antibacterial activity and low toxicity toward normal cells, we strongly believe that those nanomaterials hold great potential for use as efficient antibacterial agents.
